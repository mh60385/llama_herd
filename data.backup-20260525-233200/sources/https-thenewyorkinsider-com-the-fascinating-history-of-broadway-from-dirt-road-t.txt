The Early Days: From Lenape Trail to Great White Way Native American Roots Long before the bright lights and bustling crowds, Broadway began as a humble Lenape trail called Wickquasgeck. This trail, carved through the wilderness of Manhattan Island, was used by the native Lenape people for trade and travel. It stretched the entire length of what we now know as Manhattan, following a natural ridge that made for easier walking. When Dutch settlers arrived in the early 17th century, they recognized the trail’s strategic importance. They widened it into a road, calling it “Brede Weg” in Dutch, which translates to “Broad Way” in English. This name would stick, eventually evolving into the “Broadway” we know today. Colonial Era to Early 19th Century As New Amsterdam grew into New York , Broadway developed into a major thoroughfare. By the late 18th century, it had become the center of New York’s social scene. Wealthy residents built their homes along the street, and taverns and small entertainment venues began to appear. One of the earliest entertainment spots on Broadway was the Burns Coffee House, opened in the 1750s. It became a gathering place for artists, intellectuals, and revolutionaries, setting the stage for Broadway’s future as a cultural hub. The Birth of Broadway Theatre (1750-1900) First Theatres The history of Broadway theatre truly begins with the opening of the Theatre on Nassau Street in 1750. Located near the southern end of Broadway, it was New York’s first playhouse. Though it only operated for four years, it planted the seeds for what would become a thriving theatre district. The real game-changer came in 1798 with the opening of the Park Theatre. Located at Park Row, near the southern end of Broadway, it was the city’s first world-class theatre.
